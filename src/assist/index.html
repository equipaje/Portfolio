Assist

Micro-volunteering platform

My Role

  • Interaction Designer
  • Visual Designer
  • Researcher

Summary

Assist is a micro-volunteering platform designed to let people get help when they need it most. Assist was born from Inclusive Design practices.

Project Team

  • Brittany Bentley: Researcher, Prototyper
  • Joe Bernstein: Researcher, Project manager
  • Josh Schramm: Interaction designer, Logo designer

Duration

  • 10 weeks

Research question

How might we help someone with low or no vision navigate an unfamiliar building accurately and confidently?

Research methods

Interviews

We went to Seattle Lighthouse for the Blind and talked to people about their workplace and how they navigate new places. We also interviewed several people with vision impairments.

Observation

We watched people navigate public spaces like train stations and libraries.

Survey

We conducted a survey of people with vision disabilities.

Literature review

We read research about wayfinding and perception.

Personas

Based on our research, we created three personas to guide our design process.

Ideation & Sketching

We brainstormed solutions using our research findings and our personas. We came up with more than 50 solutions ranging from an accessibility review platform to a rolling cane that pulls users automatically toward their destination.

Pivot

After all of our sketching, we were still unsure which direction we should go in. We returned to our research findings for further analysis. Using affinity diagramming, we looked at our research through different lenses. This helped us reach a pivotal realization.


The problem that we were trying to solve was less about helping people navigate spaces than it was about getting people help when they need it most.


With this insight, we shifted focus to an application that would help people get the help they needed when they need it through a micro-volunteering platform: Assist.

Interaction Mapping

Because Assist is a multi-sided platform, it was important to lay out the interaction of both the helper and the requestor side by side to see how both experiences work together across the system.

Prototype

We created two sets of low-fidelity prototypes. The first set focused on the help request experience. The second set focused on the volunteering experience. We created paper prototypes for both experiences. For the volunteer experience, we used InVision to create an interactive prototype.


Because we wanted to be able to effectively test with our target user group, people with disabilities, we kept a physical paper prototype of the help request experience.

Usability testing

We tested both prototypes to get feedback on the concept and to understand usability of our first design. When testing with people with vision impairments, we had one team member mimic a screen reader while another swapped out screens and UI elements.

Findings

Participants liked the concept of the app and thought it would be helpful and enjoyable to use.


The design of the task time estimation screen was confusing for participants with visual impairments.


Volunteer participants were confused by the checkmark button at the bottom of the screen.


Participants with vision impairments stressed the importance of trust and safety, suggesting that we provide better ways to build relationships between volunteers and requesters.
